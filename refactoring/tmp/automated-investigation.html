Chapter I

Brussels' Blank Cheque:
The European Defence Fund

Launched in 2021, the European Defence Fund channels EU taxpayer money into military research and development. The data reveals who is collecting this public money — and what technologies they are building with it.

🔍
Key finding: Across 78 projects analysed, the EDF has disbursed over €2.3 billion to 1,657 participants. Four countries — France, Germany, Italy and Spain — capture nearly 62% of all funding, reinforcing existing industrial hierarchies rather than building new ones.

Top 10 Beneficiary Organisations

Leonardo, Italy's state-backed defence giant, leads all recipients with €172.2m across 37 projects. The German Ministry of Defence appears as a direct beneficiary — unusual for a government body — with €121.9m across just five projects. France places four organisations in the top ten.

Chapter III

The New Arsenal:
Europe's Defence Startup Boom

Across Europe, a generation of defence technology startups has emerged — building AI-powered drones, battlefield command software, and autonomous ground systems. They are backed by venture capital. And increasingly, by NATO and the EU itself.

🚀
Key finding: 18 European defence startups tracked have raised a combined total exceeding €4 billion. Helsing leads at €1.5bn. Three startups count NATO bodies among their investors, blurring the line between alliance infrastructure and private enterprise.
🌐
The transatlantic thread: General Catalyst, a major US venture firm, has invested simultaneously in Helsing, ICEYE and Alpine Eagle. Lightspeed Venture Partners, BlackRock and Andreessen Horowitz also appear. American capital is a structural force in shaping European defence technology.

Chapter IV

The Knowledge Pipeline:
Universities in the War Economy

Behind the defence companies and the startups lies a third actor: the public research establishment. European universities, national labs and research institutes are increasingly integrated into the EDF funding chain — feeding the intellectual infrastructure of autonomous weapons with public money and academic prestige.

🎓
Key finding: Research organisations received €207.2 million from the EDF — roughly 9% of total tracked funding. The Dutch institute TNO leads with €28.6m across 23 separate projects. At least 12 research bodies across 10 countries are active EDF participants.
Top Research Organisations by EDF Funding

The organisations receiving the most EDF money are not universities in the traditional sense — they are national applied-research institutes, occupying an ambiguous position between academia, industry and the state. TNO (Netherlands), AIT (Austria) and ONERA (France) lead, all with deep ties to national defence establishments.

The small-country exception

Estonia and Lithuania — small states with minimal weight in the corporate EDF rankings — punch significantly above their weight in research participation. Estonia's CR14 foundation received €7.2m; Lithuania's Baltic Advanced Technology Institute €7.6m. These are countries on NATO's eastern flank where defence has become a national priority. The EDF research track is one mechanism through which that priority is being institutionalised.

The accountability gap

The involvement of public research bodies raises questions that private investment does not. Academic institutions operate under norms of openness, peer review and public benefit. When they receive EDF funding — which funds classified and dual-use military research — these norms are tested. There is currently no systematic public registry of which universities are participating in which EDF projects, nor what their research produces.
